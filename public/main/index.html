Working through ENGWRIT 112 - A Primer

May 13, 2026

Why is English important to learn? Everyone speaks the language, don’t they? To get here, you’d obviously know how to read and write as well. So why 112? Why the stubborn insistence on an English course in your first year?

The answer lies somewhere outside white and black, instead preferring to nestle itself in the spectrum of color that is the human propensity to create. ENGWRIT 112 is a course that teaches you to accept your writing and actively work on improving it (with a few helpful pointers). However, the resolution of that wonderful creative expression we all have (yes, even you, Alex) depends on your skill with which you write.

This isn’t something you have to think of as a prerequisite to the course! However, with these 6 pointers I’m providing you now, I’m sure you’ll be able to sharpen your pen in no time.

1. Prototype. Always.

This seems self-explanatory, no? Well, I can assure you it isn’t (and I’ve been guilty of breaking this particular rule a few times). What I’m saying here is to start working on something with the *intention* of not finishing it. You don’t even have to start at the beginning! If you want, go straight to the conclusion or work on the flow between your points. This allows your mind to switch between tasks, reducing your frustration and helping develop your ideas in a more natural manner.

When prototyping, you also start to get a feel for the scope of your project. If you find that something becomes too cumbersome to work with, you have the freedom to drop or reconsider it. It’s your piece, and it always will be.

2. Get advice from the Writing Center!

As you’re taking ENGWRIT 112, I’m assuming you’re a freshman. I’d heavily recommend looking up the resources available to you! The university has a writing center in the Du Bois Library, across from the Digital Media Lab in the basement (The Writing Center : UMass Amherst), which offers you in-person, specific advice for your particular piece.

This does technically come out of your tuition, so I’d recommend going at least once if you want to get your money’s worth. Keep an eye out for an email from the Writing Center, as they usually send one out every year. (PS: This applies to the Learning Resource Center, university events and a whole host of other stuff! Check out @umassupc and @umass_scua on Instagram for some of my favorite organizations on campus.)

3. Perfection is the enemy of progress.

So you’ve worked on a first draft of your essay, research paper or multimodal project. You’ve even gone to the Writing Center and recruited a helpful volunteer in ironing out your piece. However, you’re still not satisfied with the end result.

Do you:

  1. Work day and night in order to improve your final draft?

  2. Call it a day, and let it marinate?

The correct answer is, surprisingly, the second one. It seems counterintuitive, but it’s actually due to the way the course is structured. In 112, you start by submitting your first transcript and then subject it to a review session with your peers. This is actually quite useful because your peers often see something you may have overlooked, or may appreciate something you thought would bomb.

Get something out the door, and then focus on polishing it.

4. Energy is the greatest motivator you’ll have.

No, you cannot wait until your piece is finished to eat or drink. You’ll find yourself adding in mistakes that a less famished version of you wouldn’t have. If you find yourself getting frustrated or losing motivation to write, stop. You always have enough time, and exercise is a great way to get blood flowing throughout your body. Both your mind and your body need to be in shape to work effectively.

Go out and grab a food at one of our 4 (or 5 if you count Blue Wall) dining commons. Stretch your legs for a bit. Breathe in the New England air, as frigid or dry as it may be.

5. Good artists copy. Great artists ~~steal~~ copy correctly.

Steve Jobs got it mostly right when he quoted Pablo Picasso in *Triumph of the Nerds: The Transcripts, Part III*, but in ENGWRIT 112, you can’t get away with stealing as easily. However, what you can do is cite them! Nobody’s expecting you to come up with an entirely independent line of thought, but they are expecting you to show them where to look if they want to talk with the giants you stand on the shoulders of.

In my ENGWRIT 112 course, we used the MLA 9 citation format. It’s versatile (even if I’m personally not the biggest fan), and I’ve found that the following image from EduBirdie’s MLA citation generator works wonders in understanding how to do so properly:

If you have any questions, the first thing I’d recommend is asking your professor. They’ll always have your back when it comes to things like this.

6. Interrogate yourself.

As a writer (both currently and to-be), I’d recommend internalizing the fact that Writer You and Reader You are two separate beings. While writing, it’s good practice to stop at the end of each draft (or paragraph, if that’s what you prefer) to read the whole piece over again. Go through it as if it were a text assigned for class. What points is Writer You trying to make? What’s the connection between each paragraph? Why did they choose this touchstone, and what does it signify?

If you’re able to, I’d recommend asking friends to read through your work as well. It’s good to have multiple eyes on a piece.
